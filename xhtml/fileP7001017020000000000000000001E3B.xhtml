6.1 Nominal GDP and Real GDP

  1. In 1935, Canada’s nominal GDP was about $5 billion. In 2013, it was about $1800 billion. That means that the average Canadian was about 360 times better off in 2013 compared to 1935. T  F

  2. In 1935, Canada’s real GDP was about $70 billion. In 2013, it was about $1400 billion. That means that the average Canadian was about 20 times better off in 2013 compared to 1935. T  F

  3. In 1935, Canada’s real GDP per person was about $7000. In 2013, it was about $40 000. That means that the average Canadian was about 6 times better off in 2013 compared to 1935. T  F

  4. During the 1990s, real GDP rose faster than nominal GDP. T  F

6.2 Value Added and the Enlarged Circular Flow

  1. Back in 2012, I contributed to nominal GDP by selling my used 2009 Ford truck to my friend. T  F

  2. My rich neighbour, Pablo, purchased a new Ferrari made in Italy, increasing Canadian GDP. T  F

6.3 Potential GDP and Economic Growth

  1. Back in 1911, it took more hours of work to earn enough money to buy sausages than it takes today. T  F

  2. Back in the 1970s, the cell phone was invented. This increased the quality of labour. T  F

  3. An old friend of mine, Farmer Fred, started using fertilizer back in 1941. This increased the quality of the land. T  F

  4. My sister put $1000 in a savings account in 1935 with a fixed annual interest rate of 1 percent per year, then left the account alone. With compound interest, the savings account balance reached $2000 in 2005. T  F

6.4 Business Cycles

  1. The economy expanded in the early 2000s and hit a peak in 2008. T  F

  2. Back in my youth, I remember a three quarter contraction in real GDP. This was a recession. T  F

6.5 What’s Wrong with GDP as a Measure of Well-Being?

  1. If underground economic activity were included in GDP calculations, measured GDP levels would be higher. T  F

  2. Real GDP per person in the United States is much higher than in Canada. Americans are therefore better off than Canadians. T  F

  3. If two economies have the same real GDP per person, then the overall well-being must be the same in each economy. T  F
