7.4 Inflation Starts with “M” The Quantity Theory of Money

The quantity theory of money explains inflation from an increase in the quantity of money in an economy, holding constant the velocity of money and the quantity of real output.

  • For any economy with money, M×V=P×Q, where

    • – M represents the quantity of money.

    • – V represents the velocity of money — number of times a unit of money changes hands during a year.

    • – P represents average prices — the consumer price index.

    • – Q represents the aggregate quantity of real output.

    • – P×Q represents nominal GDP.

  • There must be enough money, multiplied by the velocity of money, to allow sales of all final products and services produced (nominal GDP).

  • Quantity theory of money states that an increase in the quantity of money causes an equal percentage increase in the inflation rate.

    • – Quantity theory of money takes equation M×V=P×Q, fixes V and fixes Q at potential GDP.

    • – Quantity theory of money is behind “printing money causes inflation.”

  • Not all inflation is caused by increases in quantity of money. But inflation is always accompanied by increases in quantity of money.
