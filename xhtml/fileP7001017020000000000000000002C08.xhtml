M1+ and M2+

There are many ways to measure the supply of money in Canada. The official measures used by the Bank of Canada are called M1+ and M2+ (see Figure 9.3).

M1+ consists of currency in circulation plus demand deposits. In March 2014, there was $65 billion in currency and $661 billion in demand deposits. M1+ equaled $726 billion.

M2+ is a broader measure of the money supply. It includes all of M1+ plus all other deposits, like savings accounts and guaranteed investment certificates. M2+ was $1607 billion. These other deposits are not as liquid as demand deposits. They have restrictions or penalties for withdrawal on demand. But they are relatively easy to convert into money, which is why they are included in this broader measure of the money supply.

Notice that currency ($65 billion) is a small fraction of the money supply — about 9 percent of M1+ and 4 percent of M2+.
