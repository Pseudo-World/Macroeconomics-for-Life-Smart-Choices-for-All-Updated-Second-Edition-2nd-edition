9.1 Demand for Money

  1. It is always smart to want to hold more money. T  F

  2. If interest rates rise, it is smarter to want to hold more money. T  F

  3. If average prices increase, the demand for money increases. T  F

  4. If I hold more of my wealth as money, this guarantees my purchasing power stays the same. T  F

9.2 Supply of Money

  1. If you withdraw $50 from your bank account and put it in your wallet, the money supply increases. T  F

  2. Coins and bills are a large fraction of the money supply. T  F

  3. The quantity of money supplied depends on the interest rate. T  F

  4. If I shift money from my chequing to savings accounts, M1+ decreases and M2+ increases. T  F

9.3 Interest Rates, Money, and Bonds

  1. Bonds are less risky than money as a store of value. T  F

  2. Bonds promise to pay back the original value plus a fixed dollar amount of money. T  F

  3. When the interest rate is above the equilibrium rate, people sell bonds to get more money. T  F

9.4 Domestic Monetary Transmission Mechanism

  1. Money does not directly affect aggregate supply. T  F

  2. Lower interest rates are a negative aggregate demand shock. T  F

  1. Economists disagree about whether money helps, or slows, the economy’s adjustment to equilibrium. T  F
