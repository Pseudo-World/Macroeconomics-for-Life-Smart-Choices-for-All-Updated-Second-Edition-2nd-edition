Economics Out There: Who Wins from a Falling Loonie?

The exchange rate for the Canadian dollar fell from a high of US$1.01 in 2013 to US$0.88 in early 2014. But, as John Greenwood states in his 2014 article, “. . . depending on what you do for a living,” the falling value of the loonie can be a good thing. Yes, the depreciating dollar has been bad news for cross-border shoppers and travellers vacationing in the United States, but very good news for Canadian exporters.

According to Doug Porter, BMO Capital Markets chief economist, the falling loonie “is a blessing” for Canadian exporters and domestic tourism operators.

The “biggest winners” are businesses that sell their output in U.S. dollars while paying for their costs in Canadian dollars. They win both ways, sending their profit margins up.

Porter estimates that “a 10% drop” in the value of the Canadian dollar increases Canadian real GDP by “0.5 to 1.0 percentage points per year.” This economic growth creates Canadian jobs.

Porter believes that the government and the Bank of Canada understand “what’s in the interests of the country” overall and will not try to stop the falling loonie.
