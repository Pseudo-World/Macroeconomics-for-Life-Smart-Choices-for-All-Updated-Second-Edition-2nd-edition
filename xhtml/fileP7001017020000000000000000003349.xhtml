Current Account: Exports, Imports, and Interest and Transfer Payments

The current account measures Canada’s yearly exports and imports of products and services. To understand the pluses and minuses in Figure 10.12, focus on the flows of money into (positive) and out of (negative) Canada.

Note

Flows of Canadian dollars into Canada are positive numbers on the balance of payments accounts; flows of Canadian dollars out of Canada are negative numbers.

When Canada exports Bombardier airplanes to Germany, the German buyer pays by exchanging euros for Canadian dollars in the foreign exchange market and then sending the Canadian dollars to Bombardier in Canada. When Canada sells exports to the rest of the world, Canadian dollars flow into Canada. Flows of money into Canada are positive numbers. In 2017, Canada exported products and services worth $662 billion, bringing that amount of money into Canada — products out, money in.

When Canadians import wine from France, we pay by exchanging Canadian dollars for euros in the foreign exchange market and then sending the euros to France. When Canadians buy imports from R.O.W., Canadian dollars flow out of Canada. Flows of money out of Canada are negative numbers. In 2017, Canada imported products and services worth $711 billion from R.O.W., sending that amount of money out of Canada — products in, money out.

The other, smaller item on the current account measures net flows of interest income on investments, and labour and transfers income between Canada and R.O.W. The –$15 billion in 2017 means $15 billion more in investment/labour transfer income flowed out of Canada than into Canada.

The current account balance of –$64 billion adds up these flows of money for exports, imports and net interest and transfer payments. The negative number means Canadians sent $64 billion more to R.O.W for imports (and income and transfers) than R.O.W. sent to Canada for our exports (and income and transfers). A negative balance is a deficit. Canada had a $64 billion current account deficit in 2017.

Note

There is a current account deficit (negative balance) when Canadian spending on imports from R.O.W. is greater than R.O.W. spending on Canadian exports (and net investment/labour/transfers).
