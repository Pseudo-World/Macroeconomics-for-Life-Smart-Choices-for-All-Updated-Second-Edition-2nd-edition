Selling Bonds to Decrease the Money Supply

The same story works in reverse when the Bank of Canada wants to raise interest rates to slow the economy down. It sells bonds to decrease the money supply.

Note

To raise interest rates and slow down the economy, the Bank of Canada sells bonds, which decreases the money supply.

When the Bank of Canada sells a bond, it must be paid. As a result, cash reserves leave the chartered banking system and go to the Bank of Canada. With fewer reserves, chartered banks reduce the quantity of loans (and matching demand deposits) that they make. Suppose the chartered banks reduce loans, with matching demand deposits, by $20 billion at every interest rate. With the decrease in demand deposits, the supply of money (M1+) decreases, shifting the money supply curve leftward to S2.
