Business Investment Spending

Business investment spending is the most volatile part of aggregate demand â€” it fluctuates up and down with changes in interest rates and expectations of future profits. Businesses can easily postpone spending on new factories or additional machinery. When business investment spending decreases, there is a multiplied decrease in aggregate demand and real GDP. A collapse in investment spending between 1929 and 1933 was an important cause of the Great Depression. Investment spending dropped almost 80 percent, from $1.4 billion to $0.3 billion. The reverse is also true. Expansions are often triggered by increases in business investment spending that increase aggregate demand with a multiplied impact on GDP.
