Inflationary Gaps and Contractionary Fiscal Policy

Figure 12.5 shows an economy initially in a short-run equilibrium where aggregated demand (AD0) intersects short-run aggregate supply (SAS0) at a real GDP of $1600 billion. Potential GDP, shown by the long-run aggregate supply curve (LAS0), is $1500 billion. The economy has an inflationary gap, with real GDP $100 billion above potential GDP, and the unemployment rate is below the natural rate. Unemployment is not a problem since there are shortages of workers and rising wages, not shortages of jobs.

To decrease aggregate demand, governments can decrease spending, raise taxes, or decrease transfer payments. Contractionary fiscal policy is a negative aggregate demand shock, shifting the aggregate demand curve leftward from AD0 to AD1, decreasing real GDP, increasing unemployment, and decreasing inflation. The new equilibrium is at the intersection of AD1, SAS0, and LAS0. Real GDP is $1500 billion and the price level falls to 120, decreasing the inflation rate. Unemployment is at the natural rate.
