True/False

  1. 1. False. Since money pays no interest but bonds pay interest, sometimes it is smarter to hold less money and more bonds.

  2. 2. False. Rising interest rates increase the opportunity cost of holding money, decreasing the quantity demanded of money.

  3. 3. True. Need more money to cover more expensive purchases.

  4. 4. False. Money’s purchasing power will decrease with inflation.

  5. 5. False. Stays the same. This shifts money from one component of M1+ (demand deposits) to another (currency).

  6. 6. False. Currency is a small fraction of the money supply (4 percent of M2+).

  7. 7. True. Rise in the interest rate increase quantity of money supplied.

  8. 8. False. M1+ decreases, but since chequing and saving accounts are both part of M2+, no change in M2+.

  9. 9. False. Bonds are riskier because changes in the interest rate change the market price of bonds.

  10. 10. True. Bonds do not promise a fixed interest rate.

  11. 11. False. When interest rates are high, there is excess supply of money; people buy bonds to get rid of money.

  12. 12. True. Money affects aggregate demand.

  13. 13. False. Positive aggregate demand shock.

  14. 14. True. Camps disagree.

  15. 15. False. The “Yes — Markets Self-Adjust” camp believes external supply shocks, not money, are the main source of business cycles.
