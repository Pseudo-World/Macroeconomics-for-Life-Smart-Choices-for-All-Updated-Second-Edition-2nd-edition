True/False

  1. 1. False. Comparative advantage, specialization, and trade reduce self-sufficiency.

  2. 2. True. Trade only happens when both parties benefit.

  3. 3. False. Exports as a percentage of GDP are 30 percent for Canada and 14 percent for the United States.

  4. 4. True. For mutual benefits, terms of trade must be between each trader’s local opportunity costs.

  5. 5. True. Allows access to products not produced at home.

  6. 6. True. Trade benefits consumers.

  7. 7. True. Better products, in this case without the job loss often accompanying creative destruction.

  8. 8. False. Tariffs hurt consumers by making imports more expensive, and protect some non-competitive businesses.

  9. 9. True. Unequal distribution of gains and losses produces political pressure for protectionism to help the losers.

  10. 10. True. National security and cultural identity are valid, limited arguments for protectionism.

  11. 11. True. Hands-on economists support a limited role for government in helping losers adjust to freer trade.

  12. 12. False. Workers make smart economic choices selecting highest-paying jobs. Sweatshop jobs pay more than farm jobs.

  13. 13. False. Social safety nets are government programs to assist citizens through difficult economic transitions. Employment Insurance is an example of a Canadian social safety net.

  14. 14. True. She argues trade is not the problem.

  15. 15. True. Politicians you vote for make trade policy.
